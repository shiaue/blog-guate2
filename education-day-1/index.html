Education

"Tell me and I forget. Teach me and I remember. Involve me and I learn."
-Benjamin Franklin

Today was the first day of classes- part of me was disappointed my Guatemalan partner didn't get a group of students/teachers together in time for classes on Monday or Tuesday‚Ä¶but either way, we‚Äôre here.
Ground zero. First steps.

At the last minute, my partner told me that we weren't using the course video resources that I spent hours preparing. So as the class rolled in, I just winged it. Improv teaching... in Spanish. All my preparations became irrelevant and we just dived in.

We started off with introductions hoping to get the students comfortable. I also asked my partner to talk about "ground rules" for class:

  1. Ask questions as soon as you have any problems or confusions
  2. Be honest
  3. Be creative
  4. Be motivated
  5. Participate
  6. Work together

From what I understand, my partner and I are going to use this class as trial run for the class with all teachers.

Class Review

Wow... this was tough. I scrambled and quickly found some basic HTML example pages online and showed the class "what exactly HTML is". We started off with a basic HTML page with no styling and the majority of the class just copied "code" line by line (I personally don't even consider working with html - "coding"). Then we walked through basic elements of HTML and showed them how to open a file with Notepad++ and get started from scratch.

The shyness was palpable and I went around to the students individually to ask if they needed individual assistance.

Some definitely have the desire to learn and dive in to this, but the cultural shyness was very hard to break through. I found some students that learned very fast and were bored and I had to come by their computer to give them more challenges. I found a student who was 21 and already had web programming experience and also had 2 laptops. He's definitely going to be teaching classes when I leave.

Challenges:
Some computers were still in English (couldn't change OS display language) or had Spanish keyboard settings on a US physical keyboard laptop (this made it hard to type "<>/" characters which were crucial to HTML).

Students are great at copying, that's the majority of their education here in Patzun. (Copy what's on the board and don't ask questions; read more on Guatemalan education later)

To prevent so much copying, the example files should all be on the computer. Also HTML / CSS guides in spanish for them to copy onto a USB and take home would be helpful as well. Maybe in the future, create a local network for all the computers to connect to and then have them pull files from the server.

We need a way to gauge the level of technology literacy with the students. Maybe a pretest or something. Some of them didn't know how to open a file on the desktop. And some of them have seen HTML before.

Current State of Guatemalan Education:
Teachers know everything so the students are expected to just show up and shut up. Raising hands in class is shunned as it's embarrassing if you don't know anything so no one talks. There doesn't seem to be many challenges in school and from grade school to high school the classes only go half the day. It's called "tipo banco" meaning it's like a bank transaction, you go to the bank because they have something you don't, money. Students don't have conversations / dialogue with teachers nor ask questions. They don't actively participate in their learning process and that's something I hope this class helps them change. To take learning into their own hands. Males having female friends and vice versa are also very uncommon. In my class all the guys sat on one side of the table and the girls on the other. Crazy.

Student Feedback: At the end of class we asked the class to verbally share what they learned or any feedback for us to improve.
The class was dead silent. (It's going to take a lot to combat the Guatemalan cultural urge to stay quiet) But best thing that happened all class was when we asked the students to create a basic HTML page with their comments. What they liked / didn't like; what worked / what didn't; We asked for any constructive criticism to help us improve for the next class. It was crazy to read (and translate) some of the feedback from the students (some who came in with ZERO web dev experience).
